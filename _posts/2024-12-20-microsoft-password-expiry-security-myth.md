    <div class="content-intro">
      <p class="lead">I remember about 3 years ago my manager tapped me on the shoulder and said "You know expiring your password is actually less secure than just having a really strong password" and I noticed that this was the first place I ever worked at that didn't have a password expiration period. I was blown away and for the next 4 years working here I have never changed my password.</p>
    </div>

    <section class="content-section" id="the-myth">
      <h2>The Mind-Blowing Revelation</h2>
      <p>That conversation completely changed how I thought about password security. For years, I'd been conditioned to believe that forcing password changes every 90 days was a security best practice. Every company I'd worked for had some version of this policy - "Passwords expire every 90 days" or "You must change your password quarterly."</p>

      <p>But here I was, at a company that didn't force password changes at all. And according to my manager, this was actually more secure. My mind was blown.</p>
    </section>

    <section class="content-section" id="microsoft-research">
      <h2>Why Password Expiry Backfires</h2>
      <p>It turns out that Microsoft's research team has been saying this for years. When you force people to change passwords frequently, they do predictable things:</p>

      <ul>
        <li>Use patterns like Password1, Password2, Password3</li>
        <li>Make minimal changes like MyPassword â†’ MyPassw0rd</li>
        <li>Write passwords down because they're hard to remember</li>
        <li>Use the same password across multiple accounts</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Microsoft found that users forced to change passwords every 90 days are 2.5 times more likely to use weaker passwords than users who aren't forced to change them regularly.</p>

      <p>The logic is simple: if an attacker gets your password, they use it immediately. They don't wait around for 90 days. And if they can't get in right away, they move on to other targets.</p>
    </section>

    <section class="content-section" id="better-approaches">
      <h2>What Actually Works</h2>
      <p>Instead of forcing password changes, focus on:</p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Strong passwords from the start</strong> - 12+ characters, unique for each account</li>
        <li><strong>Multi-factor authentication (MFA)</strong> - Microsoft found this blocks 99.9% of attacks</li>
        <li><strong>Monitoring for compromised passwords</strong> - change only when there's evidence of breach</li>
        <li><strong>User education</strong> - teach people about password security best practices</li>
      </ul>
    </section>

    <section class="content-section" id="takeaway">
      <h2>My Takeaway</h2>
      <p>Four years later, I still haven't changed my password at that company. And you know what? I feel more secure than I ever did at places that forced quarterly changes.</p>

      <p>Sometimes the best security practices are the ones that work with human nature, not against it. A strong password that never changes is better than weak passwords that change every 90 days.</p>

      <p>What do you think? Have you worked somewhere that doesn't force password changes?</p>
    </section>
